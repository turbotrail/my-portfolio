Across countless tech campuses in India, there’s a familiar scene:

- 👉 Hackathons on **“patentable ideas”**
- 👉 Emails encouraging engineers to **“file IP”**
- 👉 Celebrations when someone’s idea is granted a **U.S. patent**

And the reward?

- 💵 A certificate  
- 💵 A line in an appraisal  
- 💵 Maybe a few hundred dollars  

Meanwhile, the company — almost always headquartered abroad — **owns that patent**.

It’s filed in the U.S., strengthens their global IP portfolio, and becomes part of a **multi-billion-dollar product strategy**.

---

## The uncomfortable truth

- 🧠 The ideas are born in **India**
- 📄 The ownership goes to **America**
- 💼 The economic power follows the **ownership**

And in a few years, when that technology returns to the Indian market, we often **license it**, **buy it**, or **compete with it**.

---

## This isn’t about blaming individuals

Most employees sign contracts that assign all intellectual property to the employer as a condition of employment. That’s normal. That’s legal.

But at a **national scale**, this model does something deeper:

- It transfers **strategic technological advantage** to another country  
- It keeps India positioned as a **talent provider**, not an **innovation owner**  
- It limits Indian startups from building on our own breakthroughs — because the patents are locked away overseas  

Innovation without ownership is just **outsourced R&D**.

---

## Maybe it’s time to ask tougher questions

- 🔍 Should there be more awareness about **who owns what we create**?
- 🔍 Should Indian innovators push for **joint ownership** or **local patent filings**?
- 🔍 Should companies building in India also invest in **strengthening India’s IP ecosystem** — not just their own global portfolios?

---

## A future worth building

Our engineers, researchers, and problem-solvers are among the best in the world.

Let’s make sure our ideas don’t **leave our borders** before they’ve had a chance to **build something powerful here**.

Because innovation is valuable —  
but **innovation with ownership changes nations**.
